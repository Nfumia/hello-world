Intro

Hello, my name is Nathan Fumia. I am a fourth generation California farmer and aspiring research scientist. I have broad interests and experience in all things crop improvement, including but not limited to agronomy, horticulture, integrated pest management, and variety development. Currently I reside in the beautiful state of Hawaii pursuing a Doctorate degree from the Department of Tropical Plant and Soil Sciences at the University of Hawaii at Manoa, focusing on quantitative genetics and plant breeding. Check out more about my PhD research work!

Alongside my PhD research, I am the Sustainable Agriculture Fellow with the Hawaii Agriculture Research Center where my activities are the management of the stevia (Stevia rebaudiana) breeding and domestication program, greenhouse facilities management, and breeding methodologies improvement in tropical species cacao (Theobroma cacao) and koa (Acacia koa).

Work

My current PhD dissertation work focuses on increasing agrobiodiversity through de novo domestication to offer diverse food, improve crop plant adaptability to climate change, increase production resilience, preserve healthy ecosystems, and improve soil fertility. Whilst historical domestication is well understood, the expectation and empirical evidence of newly domesticating a crop is sparse. The purpose of my dissertation is to address these two aspects, expectation and evidence. First, the theoretical expectation of the functional fixation of traits associated with the domesticated state (mono- through polygenic) in different life-histories using different breeding strategies through simulation. Second, empirical evidence of the rate of gain (reduction in additive variance) in the modern breeding program of a semi-domesticated crop will inform the simulation derived expectation. Third, develop an understanding of the shift in the amount and precision of phenotyping required depending on the placement on domestication continuum (e.g. wild, semi-domesticate, domesticate). Fourth, control of population size and selection of elite breeding individuals will have an increase in desirable trait frequency but a decrease in overall genetic diversity, this control of the bottleneck size will alter the rate of gain from selection. It is in this effort that the expectation of cost and efficiency will be better understood to facilitate wide adoption of long-term breeding strategies such as de novo domestication.

I am also a part-time contributor to the Customer Success team at iUNU, Inc. as a Horticultural Specialist where my primary role is as a data-driven (Computer-Vision) horticultural analyst improving the production methods of our clients across the horticulture industry. My main contributions are in integrated pest management techniques and applications through computer-vision technology while also participating in cultivation management techniques.

About

Crop improvement, in all its forms, is my true passion. Maximizing yield and quality of agricultural and horticultural commodities brings me enormous gratification. I grew up as a fourth generation California farmer, learning the operations of a fruit orchard production company. Following my graduation from Cal Poly, I spent a couple years crop consulting in Watsonville and Hollister, California on dozens of specialty row crops, assisting small and large farmers in integrated pest management, fertility, irrigation, and best practices. Following this I moved to San Diego, California to be production manager of a cannabis greenhouse for 3 years, leveraging technologically advanced systems. Now, I am at the University of Hawaii studying plant breeding and working with the Hawaii Agriculture Research Center on breeding and domestication of Stevia in a private-public research partnership. I also spend time working with iUNU as a horticultural specialist with the specific aim towards the success of our customers. Every facet and step of my pursuit is the improvement of agricultural and horticultural production.
